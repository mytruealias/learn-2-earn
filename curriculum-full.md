# PATH 6: Addiction & Recovery Foundations

### Lesson 6.1.1: Addiction 101 (Without Shame)

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Use, Misuse, and Disorder

Using a substance doesn't automatically mean addiction. There's a range: use (occasional), misuse (causing problems), and substance use disorder (can't stop despite harm). Understanding where you are helps you know what to do next — without shame.

**Card 2 — INFO**
> The Habit Loop

Every habit follows a loop: Trigger (stress, boredom, people) → Behavior (the substance) → Reward (relief, numbness, energy). Addiction hijacks this loop by making the reward feel essential. Breaking the loop starts with seeing it clearly.

**Card 1 — INFO**

A craving feels like it will last forever, but it won't. Cravings are like ocean waves — they build, they peak, and they pass. Most cravings peak within 15-20 minutes and then start to fade. You don't have to act on them.

**Card 2 — INFO**

## Unit 6.3: Craving & Stress Tools

### Lesson 6.3.1: Urge Surfing

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Ride the Wave, Don't Fight It

Urge surfing means noticing a craving without acting on it. Observe it like a wave: it starts small, builds to a peak, and then it falls. You don't have to fight it — just watch it pass. Most waves last 15-30 minutes.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What's the correct order of urge surfing?

○ Fight it, suppress it, ignore it
✅ Notice it, observe it build, watch it peak, let it fade
○ Give in quickly to make it stop
○ Distract yourself immediately

*Explanation: Urge surfing is about observation, not resistance. Watch the wave. It always comes down.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Ride One Wave

The next time a craving hits, try urge surfing. Note the time it starts, when it peaks, and when it fades. You'll be surprised — most cravings pass faster than you think.

### Lesson 6.3.2: Delay and Distract

**Card 1 — INFO**
> 15 Minutes Can Change Everything

When a craving hits, delay acting on it for just 15 minutes. Use that time to do something — anything — else. Walk, shower, call someone, listen to music, eat a snack. Most cravings fade in that window.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**

✅ Go for a quick walk and drink cold water

*Explanation: Physical movement and cold water engage your body and shift your brain's focus. It's simple and effective.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Build Your 15-Minute List

Write down 5 things you can do for 15 minutes when a craving hits: walk, shower, call someone, listen to music, eat a healthy snack. Keep this list on your phone.

### Lesson 6.3.3: Thought Traps

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Your Brain Lies to You Sometimes

Thought traps are patterns that keep you stuck. Common ones: All-or-nothing ('I messed up once so I might as well give up'), Catastrophizing ('Everything is ruined'), and the 'I already blew it' trap. Catching these traps is half the battle.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**

○ Good planning
✅ All-or-nothing thinking
○ Realistic assessment
○ Positive self-talk

**Card 3 — INFO**

Take the trapped thought and rewrite it as a fair thought. 'I already blew it' becomes 'I had a setback, but I can make a different choice right now.' Fair thoughts are honest AND hopeful.

**Card 4 — INFO**

Think of one thought trap you've fallen into recently. Write the trapped thought, then rewrite it as a fair, balanced thought. Keep both — seeing the difference builds the skill.

### Lesson 6.3.4: Grounding and Reset

**Card 1 — INFO**
> The 5-4-3-2-1 Technique

When anxiety or cravings spike, ground yourself: Name 5 things you see, 4 things you can touch, 3 things you hear, 2 things you smell, 1 thing you taste. This pulls your brain out of panic mode and back to the present moment.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**

✅ To bring your attention back to the present moment
○ To make the problem go away

*Explanation: Grounding reconnects you to right here, right now. You can't solve anything from panic mode — grounding gets you back to thinking mode.*

**Card 3 — INFO**

The next time you feel stressed or triggered, try a 2-minute reset: 1 minute of slow breathing (in for 4, out for 6), then 1 minute of 5-4-3-2-1 grounding. Practice it now so it's ready when you need it.

# PATH 7: Harm Reduction & Safety

## Unit 7.1: Safety Basics

### Lesson 7.1.1: Harm Reduction Mindset

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Safer Is Better

Harm reduction means any step that makes things safer — even if you're not ready to stop. Using less, using in safer ways, not using alone, carrying naloxone — every safety upgrade counts. No shame, no judgment, just safer choices.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What does harm reduction mean?

○ You have to quit completely or it doesn't count
✅ Any step that reduces risk counts as progress
○ It means you don't care about recovery
○ It only works for some substances

*Explanation: Harm reduction meets you where you are. Any reduction in risk is a win — period.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Choose 1 Safety Upgrade

Pick one thing you can do this week to be safer: don't use alone, carry naloxone, use less, test your supply, or have an emergency plan. One upgrade. That's all.

### Lesson 7.1.2: Overdose Basics

**Card 1 — INFO**
> What Overdose Is

An overdose happens when your body can't handle the amount of substance. Mixing substances (like opioids and alcohol, or opioids and benzodiazepines) dramatically increases risk. Signs include slow or stopped breathing, blue lips, unresponsiveness, and gurgling sounds.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Which combination creates the HIGHEST overdose risk?

○ Coffee and water
✅ Opioids and alcohol together
○ Vitamins and food
○ Walking and stretching

*Explanation: Mixing depressants (opioids + alcohol) is extremely dangerous because both slow breathing. Together, they can stop it entirely.*

**Card 3 — TRUE_FALSE**
> Someone who is overdosing can always be woken up by cold water or slapping.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: Cold water and slapping don't reverse an overdose. If someone can't be woken up, call 911 immediately and use naloxone if available.*

**Card 4 — INFO**
> Mission: My Emergency Plan Card

Create a simple card (phone note or paper) with: 1) Call 911, 2) Give naloxone if available, 3) Put person in recovery position, 4) Stay with them. Save this card on your phone now.

### Lesson 7.1.3: Naloxone Basics

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Naloxone Saves Lives

Naloxone (Narcan) is a medicine that reverses opioid overdoses. It's safe, it works fast, and it has no effect if the person hasn't taken opioids. Many pharmacies carry it without a prescription. The CDC recommends treating any suspected overdose as an overdose.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> When should you use naloxone?

○ Only if you're 100% sure it's an opioid overdose
✅ Whenever you suspect an opioid overdose — it won't hurt if you're wrong
○ Only if a doctor is present
○ Never, it's dangerous

*Explanation: Naloxone is safe to give even if you're not sure. If it's not opioids, it simply won't do anything. When in doubt, give it.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Find Naloxone Near You

Search for free naloxone in your area. Many health departments, harm reduction organizations, and pharmacies provide it free or at low cost. SAMHSA's helpline (1-800-662-4357) can help you find local resources.

### Lesson 7.1.4: Calling for Help

**Card 1 — INFO**
> What to Say When You Call 911

Keep it simple: 'Someone is not breathing' or 'Someone is unconscious and won't wake up.' Give the address. Stay on the line. You don't have to mention substances if you're afraid — just describe what you see.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What's the MOST important thing to say on a 911 call for a suspected overdose?

○ List every substance they took
✅ The person's location and that they're not breathing
○ Your own name and information
○ Ask if you'll get in trouble

*Explanation: Location and symptoms save lives. That's what dispatchers need first. Everything else can come later.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Emergency Script

Save this in your phone notes: 'I need an ambulance at [ADDRESS]. Someone is not breathing/unconscious. Please hurry.' Having it ready means you won't freeze in a crisis.

### Lesson 7.1.5: Recovery Position

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Keep Them Safe Until Help Arrives

If someone is unconscious but breathing: Roll them on their side (recovery position), tilt their head back slightly to keep airway open, stay with them, and keep checking their breathing. This prevents choking if they vomit.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What's the correct order for helping someone who is unconscious?

○ Leave them alone and call later
✅ Call 911, give naloxone if available, put in recovery position, stay with them
○ Shake them hard and pour water on them
○ Drive them to the hospital yourself

*Explanation: Call 911 first, then naloxone, then recovery position, then stay. This sequence gives them the best chance.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Learn It, Teach It

Practice putting someone in the recovery position (use a pillow or ask a friend). Once you know it, consider teaching one other person. One skill shared could save a life.

## Unit 7.2: High-Risk Moments

### Lesson 7.2.1: Mixing Risks

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Mixing Multiplies Danger

Combining substances doesn't just add risk — it multiplies it. Opioids + alcohol, stimulants + depressants, anything + unknown pills — these combinations are the leading cause of overdose death. Know your combinations and avoid the deadliest ones.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Why is mixing substances more dangerous than using one alone?

○ It's actually safer to mix
✅ Different substances can amplify each other's effects on your body unpredictably
○ Mixing has no additional risk
○ Your body can handle more when you mix

*Explanation: Substances interact in unpredictable ways. What your body can handle separately, it may not survive together.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Know Your Risk Combos

Identify your top 2 risky combinations to avoid. If you're not sure, the most dangerous are: opioids + alcohol, opioids + benzodiazepines, and any substance + unknown pills.

### Lesson 7.2.2: Using Alone

**Card 1 — INFO**
> The Buddy System Can Save Your Life

Using alone is the biggest single risk factor for fatal overdose. If something goes wrong, there's no one to call for help. If you are using, having someone who can check on you could be the difference between life and death.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What's the safest approach if you're going to use?

○ Use alone so nobody judges you
✅ Have someone nearby or a check-in buddy
○ Use as much as possible quickly

*Explanation: A check-in buddy doesn't have to approve of what you're doing — they just need to be able to call for help if needed.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Check-In Plan

If you're currently using, set up a check-in buddy system: text someone before and after, or use a timed check-in. If they don't hear back, they know to check on you.

### Lesson 7.2.3: Tolerance Changes

**Card 1 — INFO**
> After a Break, Your Tolerance Drops

If you've been in treatment, jail, the hospital, or just haven't used in a while, your tolerance drops fast. Going back to your old dose after a break is one of the most common causes of overdose. Start much lower than before.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> After being sober for 2 weeks, your old dose is still safe.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: Even a few days of not using can significantly lower your tolerance. Your old dose could be fatal. Always start much lower after a break.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Risk After a Break Reminder

If you take a break (by choice or not), set a phone reminder: 'My tolerance is lower now. Start small.' This simple reminder could save your life.

### Lesson 7.2.4: Party and Social Safety

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Social Situations Are High-Risk

Parties, gatherings, and social pressure create some of the riskiest moments. Having rules before you go protects you: set a drink/use limit, have a leave plan, stay hydrated, and go with a buddy who knows your limits.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**

**Card 3 — INFO**

Choose 2 rules for your next social situation: set a limit, stay hydrated, bring a buddy, have a leave plan, eat before you go. Write them down before you go out.

### Lesson 7.2.5: After an Overdose Event

**Card 1 — INFO**

After an overdose event (yours or someone else's), the next 24 hours are critical. Rest, hydrate, and don't use again immediately — your body is vulnerable. Connect with someone: a support person, a counselor, or a helpline.

# PATH 8: Treatment & Recovery Navigation

## Unit 8.1: Treatment Types & What to Expect

### Lesson 8.1.1: Levels of Care 101

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Treatment Comes in Many Forms

Detox (medical withdrawal support), outpatient (visits while living at home), intensive outpatient (more hours), residential (live-in programs), medication-assisted treatment (MAT/MOUD), and counseling. The right level depends on YOUR needs, not a one-size-fits-all approach.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What's the difference between outpatient and residential treatment?

○ They're the same thing
✅ Outpatient means you go home after sessions; residential means you live there
○ Residential is always better
○ Outpatient means you never see anyone

*Explanation: Both are effective — it depends on your situation. Outpatient works well if you have stable housing; residential provides more structure and support.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)

Medications like buprenorphine, methadone, and naltrexone can reduce cravings and prevent withdrawal. The CDC supports expanding access to these medications. MAT is not 'trading one addiction for another' — it's evidence-based medicine that saves lives.

### Lesson 8.1.2: What a First Appointment Looks Like

**Card 1 — INFO**
> It's Just a Conversation

Your first appointment is usually a conversation, not a test. They'll ask about your substance use, mental health, medical history, and what you want help with. You don't have to have all the answers. Being honest helps them help you better.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What should you bring to a first treatment appointment?

○ Nothing, they'll figure it out
✅ ID, insurance card (if you have one), list of medications, and your questions
○ A list of excuses
○ Someone to answer for you

*Explanation: Being prepared shows you're serious and helps the provider help you faster. If you don't have insurance, tell them — many programs work with uninsured people.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Write 3 Questions

Before your first appointment, write down 3 questions you want to ask: What will treatment look like? How long does it take? What if I have a setback? Having questions shows you're engaged and helps you make informed choices.

### Lesson 8.1.3: Confidentiality Basics

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Your Treatment Is Usually Private

Federal law protects substance use treatment records. Generally, what you share with your treatment provider stays private. There are some exceptions (immediate danger, child safety concerns), but overall your information is protected.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> Treatment providers can share your substance use information with anyone they want.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: Federal confidentiality laws (42 CFR Part 2) provide strong protections for substance use treatment records. Your privacy is taken seriously.*

**Card 3 — INFO**
> Mission: Comfort Checklist

Write a short list of what you're comfortable sharing and what you'd rather keep private for now. This helps you feel more in control during appointments. Remember: you can always share more later as trust builds.

### Lesson 8.1.4: Support Groups Overview

**Card 1 — INFO**
> You Don't Have to Go Alone

Support groups come in many formats: 12-step (AA, NA), SMART Recovery, Refuge Recovery, peer support groups, and more. Some are in-person, some are online. You don't have to speak if you're not ready — just showing up counts.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What if you go to a support group meeting and it doesn't feel right?

○ Groups don't work for anyone
✅ Try a different group — there are many types and each has a different feel
○ Never try again
○ Force yourself to keep going to that one

# PATH 9: Relapse Prevention Mastery

## Unit 9.1: Relapse Is a Process, Not a Moment

### Lesson 9.1.1: The Relapse Road

**Card 1 — INFO**
> It Starts Long Before the First Use

Relapse follows a road: risky thoughts ('One time won't hurt') → risky behaviors (going to old places, skipping meetings) → use. By the time you use, you've already passed several warning signs. Catching it early is the skill.

*Explanation: Relapse starts in the mind with thoughts like 'I can handle it now' or 'One time won't matter.' Catching these thoughts early stops the road.*

### Lesson 9.1.2: Warning Signs

**Card 1 — INFO**

✅ Starting to skip meetings and isolate yourself

### Lesson 9.1.3: The 3-Choice Rule (Pause, Reach, Replace)

**Card 1 — INFO**
> When Temptation Hits, You Have 3 Choices

PAUSE: Stop and breathe for 60 seconds. REACH: Call or text your support person. REPLACE: Do your alternative activity. You don't need all three — any ONE of these can break the cycle. But having all three as options is powerful.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> You're at a party and someone offers you a substance. What's the best first move?

*Explanation: Pausing creates space between trigger and response. That space is where your power lives.*

### Lesson 9.1.4: Recovery Scripts

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Have Your Lines Ready

Rehearsed responses work better than improvised ones. Scripts for common situations: 'No thanks, I'm good.' 'I don't do that anymore.' 'I'm driving tonight.' Short, confident, and no need to explain. Repeat if they push. Then exit.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**

○ Give in to stop the pressure

○ Get aggressive

**Card 3 — INFO**

### Lesson 9.1.5: Lapse vs Relapse (Recover Fast)

**Card 1 — INFO**
> A Lapse Is Data, Not Identity

A lapse is a single slip. A relapse is a return to old patterns. The difference is what you do NEXT. A lapse doesn't erase your progress — it teaches you something about your triggers, your plan, and what needs strengthening.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**

Write a 'reset plan' for if/when a lapse happens: 1) Stop immediately, 2) Who will I call? 3) What triggered it? 4) What will I do differently? Having this plan means a lapse stays a lapse, not a relapse.

*Explanation: Broken record technique: keep it short, keep it confident, repeat if needed. You don't need their permission to protect yourself.*

### Lesson 10.1.4: Sleep, Anxiety, and the Alcohol Cycle

**Card 1 — INFO**
> The Cycle: Anxiety → Drink → Bad Sleep → More Anxiety

Alcohol might help you fall asleep, but it destroys sleep quality. Poor sleep increases anxiety. More anxiety drives more drinking. Breaking this cycle starts with addressing the sleep and anxiety directly — not through the bottle.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> Alcohol helps you get better quality sleep.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: Alcohol disrupts REM sleep, the restorative phase your brain needs. You might pass out, but you don't rest. Better sleep strategies beat a nightcap every time.*

### Lesson 10.1.5: Alcohol Relapse Prevention

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Your Long-Term Alcohol Plan

Build your plan: know your triggers, have refusal scripts ready, keep a sober support person on speed dial, remove alcohol from your home, find alcohol-free social activities, and attend regular support meetings. Consistency beats intensity.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Which is the most important long-term strategy for alcohol recovery?

○ Willpower alone
✅ Consistent routines, support connections, and trigger management
○ Avoiding all social situations forever
○ Switching to a 'safer' substance

*Explanation: Long-term recovery is built on systems, not willpower. Routines, support, and awareness work together to keep you safe.*

## Unit 10.2: Opioid Safety & Recovery

### Lesson 10.2.1: Opioid Overdose Risk

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Opioid Overdose Is Preventable

The CDC emphasizes that naloxone is a lifesaving medicine that reverses opioid overdose. Key risks: using alone, mixing with other depressants, returning to use after a tolerance break, and unknown potency. Carry naloxone. Tell someone. Have a plan.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> After leaving jail or treatment, your old opioid dose is:

○ Still safe because your body remembers
✅ Potentially fatal because your tolerance has dropped
○ Actually lower than what you need
○ The same as before

*Explanation: Tolerance drops quickly. Your old dose after a break can be a lethal dose. Always start much lower.*

### Lesson 10.2.2: Treatment Options Including MOUD

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Medications That Help

Medications for Opioid Use Disorder (MOUD) include buprenorphine (Suboxone), methadone, and naltrexone (Vivitrol). The CDC supports expanding access to these treatments. They reduce cravings, prevent withdrawal, and cut overdose risk significantly. Ask your provider about them.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> Taking medication for opioid use disorder means you're 'not really sober.'

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: MOUD is evidence-based medicine, not 'trading one addiction for another.' People on medication live longer, healthier lives. That's the definition of working.*

### Lesson 10.2.3: Cravings, Pain, and Alternatives

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Pain Management Without Opioids

Chronic pain and opioid recovery are complicated. Talk to your provider about non-opioid pain options: physical therapy, NSAIDs, nerve blocks, mindfulness, and exercise. Managing pain is important — doing it safely is the goal.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> You're in opioid recovery and have chronic back pain. What's the best approach?

○ Take opioid painkillers and hope you can control it
✅ Work with your doctor on a non-opioid pain management plan
○ Just suffer through the pain
○ Ask a friend for their leftover pills

*Explanation: Your doctor needs to know you're in recovery. Together, you can find pain solutions that don't risk relapse.*

### Lesson 10.2.4: Recovery Supports and Re-Entry

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Planning for Transitions

Leaving treatment, getting out of jail, moving to a new city — transitions are high-risk moments. Plan ahead: where will you live, who will support you, how will you continue treatment, and what's your plan for the first 72 hours? Those first days matter most.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> You're leaving a treatment program tomorrow. What's the most important thing to have in place?

○ A going-away party
✅ A plan for safe housing, continued support, and how to handle cravings in the first week
○ A new phone
○ Nothing — you'll figure it out

*Explanation: The transition from treatment to the real world is the highest risk period. Planning ahead dramatically improves outcomes.*

### Lesson 10.2.5: Long-Term Opioid Recovery

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Recovery Is a Long Game

Long-term opioid recovery includes ongoing support (meetings, counseling), medication management if on MOUD, regular health check-ups, and building a life worth staying sober for. It gets easier, but it never stops being important.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> After a year of successful opioid recovery, should you stop all support?

○ Yes — a year is enough
✅ No — continued support (even reduced) helps maintain long-term recovery
○ Support is only for the first month
○ Only if you feel like it

*Explanation: Recovery is maintenance, not a finish line. Continued support — even monthly check-ins — keeps your foundation strong.*

## Unit 10.3: Stimulant Safety & Recovery

### Lesson 10.3.1: The Sleep Deprivation Spiral

**Card 1 — INFO**
> No Sleep, More Problems

Stimulants keep you awake, and sleep deprivation causes paranoia, anxiety, poor decisions, and physical breakdown. The cycle feeds itself: use → no sleep → feel terrible → use more to function. Breaking the cycle starts with prioritizing sleep above everything else.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> Sleep deprivation from stimulant use can cause paranoia and hallucinations.

✅ True
○ False

*Explanation: Extended sleep deprivation — especially combined with stimulant use — can cause serious mental health symptoms. Sleep is not optional for your brain.*

### Lesson 10.3.2: Crash Days Recovery

**Card 1 — INFO**
> The Crash Is Your Body Demanding Rest

After stimulant use, the 'crash' (extreme fatigue, depression, hunger) is your body trying to recover. Don't fight it — support it: sleep as much as you need, eat nutritious food, drink water, and avoid making big decisions until you've rested.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> During a stimulant crash, what's the priority?

○ Using more to feel normal again
✅ Sleep, food, water, and rest without judgment
○ Exercise intensely
○ Going to work immediately

*Explanation: Your body is recovering. Give it what it needs: rest, nutrition, and hydration. Everything else can wait.*

### Lesson 10.3.3: Triggers and High-Risk Hours

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Stimulant Use Often Has a Schedule

Many people have specific 'high-risk hours' — late nights, weekends, payday, or after stressful events. Knowing when you're most vulnerable lets you plan protection: activities, support check-ins, or simply not being alone during those hours.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Your highest-risk time is Friday nights after 10pm. What's the best strategy?

○ Hope you won't feel triggered
✅ Pre-plan a safe activity or be with a safe person during those hours
○ Stay up all night scrolling your phone
○ Go to the places where you usually use

*Explanation: Fill the high-risk window with safety. A planned Friday night is a protected Friday night.*

### Lesson 10.3.4: Rebuilding Energy and Confidence

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Natural Energy Takes Time to Rebuild

Stimulants hijack your brain's energy system. After stopping, you may feel flat, tired, and unmotivated for weeks. This is normal. Your brain is healing. Small amounts of exercise, sunlight, regular meals, and patience will gradually restore your natural energy.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Two weeks after quitting stimulants, you have zero motivation. What does this mean?

○ Recovery isn't working
✅ Your brain is healing and natural motivation will return gradually
○ You should go back to using
○ You'll feel like this forever

*Explanation: Low motivation after stimulant use is temporary. Your brain's reward system is resetting. It takes time, but it DOES come back.*

### Lesson 10.3.5: Stimulant Relapse Prevention

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Stimulant Recovery Needs Structure

Stimulant relapse often starts with boredom, nostalgia for the 'high energy' feeling, or social pressure. Prevention: fill your schedule, develop natural energy sources (exercise, sleep routine), maintain support connections, and have a plan for your high-risk hours.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What's the biggest relapse risk for stimulant recovery?

○ Too much structure
✅ Boredom and unstructured time, especially during former high-risk hours
○ Eating too healthy
○ Sleeping too much

*Explanation: Stimulant cravings love boredom. Fill your time with purpose and your high-risk hours with safety plans.*

# PATH 11: Supporter & Family Skills

## Unit 11.1: Understanding & Compassion

### Lesson 11.1.1: Addiction Basics for Supporters

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Understanding Changes Everything

Addiction is a brain condition, not a choice or moral failure. Your loved one isn't choosing to hurt you — they're trapped in a cycle their brain is driving. Understanding this doesn't excuse harmful behavior, but it changes how you respond to it.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> Addiction is simply a matter of willpower.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: Addiction changes brain chemistry. Willpower alone is rarely enough. That's why treatment, support, and coping skills matter so much.*

### Lesson 11.1.2: What Helps vs What Harms

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Good Intentions Can Go Wrong

Helping means supporting their recovery. Enabling means removing consequences that might motivate change. Paying their bills, making excuses, or ignoring the problem can feel like love but often keeps the cycle going.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Your loved one spent their rent money on substances and asks you to cover it. What's the most helpful response?

○ Pay it immediately — you can't let them be homeless
✅ Express care, offer to help them find resources, but don't cover it without accountability
○ Lecture them for an hour
○ Never speak to them again

*Explanation: Love with accountability. Help them find solutions without removing the natural consequences that can motivate change.*

### Lesson 11.1.3: Shame vs Accountability

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Shame Drives People Away From Help

Shaming someone ('You're a disgrace') pushes them deeper into the cycle. Accountability ('I love you AND this behavior isn't acceptable') keeps the door open while being honest. The goal is honesty without cruelty.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Which statement shows accountability without shame?

○ You're a terrible person and I'm done with you
✅ I love you, and I'm worried about what's happening. I can't pretend it's okay.
○ Whatever you want to do is fine with me
○ If you really loved me, you'd stop

*Explanation: Honest, caring, and direct. This keeps the relationship open while being truthful about what you see.*

### Lesson 11.1.4: Talking Without Escalation

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Timing and Tone Matter

Don't try to have a serious conversation when either of you is angry, intoxicated, or in crisis. Choose a calm moment. Use 'I' statements: 'I'm scared when...' instead of 'You always...' Keep it short. One topic at a time.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> When is the best time to talk to your loved one about their substance use?

○ Right after they've been using
✅ During a calm, private moment when you're both sober
○ In front of other people to embarrass them into changing
○ Over text while you're angry

*Explanation: Calm moments produce the best conversations. Private settings feel safer. Both being sober means both can think clearly.*

### Lesson 11.1.5: Safety Planning for Supporters

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Your Safety Comes First

If your loved one's behavior puts you or others in danger, your safety is the priority. Have a plan: a safe place to go, someone to call, and clear lines you won't allow to be crossed. Love doesn't require you to be unsafe.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> Supporting someone with addiction means accepting unsafe behavior.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: You can love someone AND refuse to be put in danger. Safety boundaries are not abandonment — they're survival.*

## Unit 11.2: Boundaries & Communication

### Lesson 11.2.1: Boundary Scripts for Supporters

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Boundaries Are Acts of Love

Scripts: 'I love you, but I won't give you money for that.' 'I'm here when you're ready for help, but I can't watch you destroy yourself.' 'I need to take care of myself too.' Boundaries protect both of you.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Your loved one says 'If you really loved me, you'd help me.' What's a good boundary response?

○ You're right, I'll do whatever you want
✅ I love you enough to say no to this. I'll help you find treatment, but I won't support the addiction.
○ Fine, I don't love you then
○ Don't talk to me about love

*Explanation: Love and limits can coexist. Offering real help while refusing to enable is one of the hardest and most loving things you can do.*

### Lesson 11.2.2: Money and Housing Boundaries

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Money Is the Most Common Enabling Tool

Giving money to someone in active addiction almost always funds the addiction, even if that's not the intent. Alternatives: pay their bills directly, buy groceries, offer transportation — give support in forms that can't be converted to substances.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Your loved one needs groceries. What's the safest way to help?

○ Give them cash
✅ Buy groceries and deliver them directly
○ Ignore the request entirely
○ Give them your credit card

*Explanation: Direct support (buying the groceries yourself) ensures your help goes where it's intended. It's practical love with protection.*

### Lesson 11.2.3: Kids and Family Safety

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Children's Safety Is Non-Negotiable

Children should never be in unsafe situations due to a family member's substance use. If children are at risk, their safety comes first — every time. Age-appropriate honesty helps kids understand without taking on adult responsibilities.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> It's better to hide a family member's addiction from children entirely.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: Children usually know something is wrong. Age-appropriate honesty reduces their confusion and self-blame. Hiding it completely can cause more harm.*

### Lesson 11.2.4: Crisis Calls as a Supporter

**Card 1 — INFO**
> When to Call for Help

Call 911 if someone is: unconscious, not breathing, having a seizure, or threatening harm to themselves or others. Call 988 for mental health crises. You are not 'betraying' them by calling for help — you might be saving their life.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Your loved one is unconscious and you suspect an overdose. What should you do?

○ Wait to see if they wake up
✅ Call 911 immediately, give naloxone if available, and put them in recovery position
○ Try home remedies first
○ Call a friend for advice before calling 911

*Explanation: Every second matters in an overdose. Call 911 first. You can't 'overdose' on calling for help.*

### Lesson 11.2.5: After a Setback (For Supporters)

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Setbacks Are Part of the Process

When your loved one relapses, it's devastating. But relapse is common in recovery and doesn't mean failure. Your role: express care, maintain your boundaries, and encourage them to get back to their recovery plan. Don't enable, but don't abandon.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Your loved one just relapsed after 60 days of sobriety. What's the most helpful response?

○ Tell them you're done and walk away forever
✅ Express your care, remind them of their progress, and encourage them to reconnect with their recovery plan
○ Pretend it didn't happen
○ Punish them

*Explanation: 60 days of sobriety isn't erased by a relapse. Encourage them to learn from it and get back on track. Your support can be the bridge back.*

### Lesson 11.2.6: Guilt and Grief for Supporters

**Card 1 — INFO**
> You Didn't Cause This

Supporter guilt is real: 'Did I cause this? Could I have prevented it? Am I doing enough?' The answer: You didn't cause it, you can't control it, and you can't cure it. What you CAN do is support recovery, set boundaries, and take care of yourself.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> A family member's addiction is usually caused by something the supporter did wrong.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: Addiction is caused by a complex mix of genetics, environment, trauma, and brain chemistry. It's not your fault.*

## Unit 11.3: Helping Them Get Help

### Lesson 11.3.1: Treatment Options for Supporters

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Know What's Available

Research options before they're needed: detox programs, outpatient and residential treatment, medication-assisted treatment, and support groups. SAMHSA's helpline (1-800-662-4357) can help you find local options. Being prepared means you can act quickly when the window opens.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**

○ Only after they ask for help
✅ Before they ask — be ready for when the window opens
○ Never — it's not your job
○ After they've already been through treatment once

*Explanation: The window of willingness can open and close fast. Having options ready means you can act immediately when they say 'I'm ready.'*

### Lesson 11.3.2: Appointment Prep for Supporters

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Help With the Logistics

Offer concrete help: 'I'll drive you to the appointment,' 'I'll help you gather your documents,' 'I'll sit in the waiting room if you want company.' Practical support removes barriers that keep people from getting help.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What's the most helpful way to support someone going to their first treatment appointment?

○ Tell them what to say
✅ Offer a ride, help with paperwork, and let them lead the conversation with the provider
○ Go in and speak for them

*Explanation: Support their autonomy while removing barriers. They need to own their recovery, but they don't have to navigate logistics alone.*

### Lesson 11.3.3: Follow-Up and Encouragement

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Check In Without Checking Up

There's a difference between supportive check-ins ('How are you feeling today?') and surveillance ('Are you using again?'). Trust-building check-ins show you care without making them feel monitored. Celebrate small wins and be patient with the process.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Which check-in approach builds trust?

○ Searching their room every day
✅ Asking 'How are you doing today? Anything you need?' genuinely
○ Testing them constantly
○ Ignoring them completely

*Explanation: Genuine interest without suspicion builds the trust that recovery needs. They'll be more honest with someone who isn't treating them like a suspect.*

### Lesson 11.3.4: Support Groups for Families

**Card 1 — INFO**
> You Need Support Too

Al-Anon, Nar-Anon, SMART Recovery Family & Friends, and other support groups exist for people like you. They're filled with people who understand exactly what you're going through. You don't have to do this alone.

**Card 2 — TRUE_FALSE**
> Support groups for families are only useful if your loved one is in recovery.

○ True
✅ False

*Explanation: Family support groups help YOU regardless of where your loved one is in their journey. Your wellbeing matters independent of their choices.*

## Unit 11.4: Supporter Self-Care

### Lesson 11.4.1: Burnout Prevention

**Card 1 — INFO**
> You Can't Pour From an Empty Cup

Supporting someone with addiction is exhausting. Burnout shows up as: resentment, hopelessness, physical symptoms, isolation, and emotional numbness. You deserve rest, fun, and your own life. Taking care of yourself isn't selfish — it's essential.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> Which is a sign of supporter burnout?

○ Feeling energized and hopeful
✅ Constant resentment, exhaustion, and feeling like nothing you do matters
○ Having healthy boundaries
○ Taking time for yourself

*Explanation: Burnout is real and damaging. If you recognize these signs, it's time to prioritize your own care and seek support.*

### Lesson 11.4.2: Building Your Own Support Team

**Card 1 — INFO**
> Supporters Need Support

You need people who understand what you're going through: a trusted friend, a therapist, a support group, or a family counselor. Don't carry this alone. Your wellbeing directly affects your ability to help your loved one.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**

✅ A combination of friends, support group, and potentially a therapist
○ Only talk to the person with addiction about it
○ Social media support groups only

*Explanation: Multiple support sources prevent any one person from being overwhelmed. Spread the weight so no one breaks.*

### Lesson 11.4.3: Your Long-Term Plan

**Card 1 — INFO**
> This Is a Marathon, Not a Sprint

Supporting someone in recovery is long-term. You need sustainable practices: regular self-care, maintained boundaries, ongoing support for yourself, and the understanding that you can love someone without losing yourself in their struggle.

**Card 2 — MULTIPLE_CHOICE**
> What makes support sustainable over the long term?

○ Giving everything you have until you're empty
✅ Maintaining boundaries, self-care, and your own support network
○ Ignoring the problem and hoping it goes away
○ Controlling every aspect of their recovery

*Explanation: Sustainable support means taking care of yourself SO THAT you can continue to be there. Balance is everything.*
